Abstract¶

The goal of this project is to develop a multimodal machine learning framework for classifying physiological states — acute stress, aerobic exercise, and anaerobic exercise — using wearable sensor data sourced from the PhysioNet Wearable Device Dataset (Hongn et al., 2025). The dataset contains synchronized time-series signals recorded with the Empatica E4 wristband, including blood volume pulse (BVP), electrodermal activity (EDA), three-axis accelerometry (ACC), skin temperature (TEMP), heart rate (HR), and inter-beat intervals (IBI), collected from healthy volunteers under controlled laboratory conditions. Signals are recorded at heterogeneous sampling frequencies (64 Hz BVP, 32 Hz ACC, 4 Hz EDA and TEMP) and require non-trivial temporal alignment, session segmentation, subject-level normalization, and fixed-length window construction before modeling. We implement a custom physiological feature extraction pipeline computing heart rate variability metrics (RMSSD, SDNN, pNN50), electrodermal statistics, motion-based features, and temperature dynamics. Three classical machine learning classifiers — Logistic Regression, Random Forest, and Gradient Boosting — are trained and compared under leave-one-subject-out cross-validation to assess generalization to unseen individuals. Results are evaluated via accuracy, macro-averaged precision, recall, F1-score, and confusion matrices, providing a rigorous comparison of model performance across the three physiological state classes.

Keywords: physiological state classification, wearable sensor data, heart rate variability, electrodermal activity, leave-one-subject-out validation, multimodal machine learning

1. Introduction¶

Wearable biosensor technology has enabled continuous, non-invasive monitoring of physiological signals in free-living environments. Devices such as the Empatica E4 capture multiple synchronized biosignals that reflect the state of the autonomic nervous system, cardiovascular activity, and musculoskeletal effort. Automatically classifying physiological states from these signals has broad clinical and practical significance: it enables adaptive health monitoring, informs chronic disease management systems, and supports real-time feedback for athletic training.

Three physiological states are of particular relevance in this work. Acute stress is a psychological and neurophysiological response to cognitive or emotional challenge, mediated primarily by the sympathetic nervous system. It produces characteristic increases in electrodermal activity, heart rate elevation, and suppression of heart rate variability. Aerobic exercise involves sustained, rhythmic physical effort at moderate-to-high intensity, producing progressive cardiovascular demand, gradual temperature rise from thermoregulatory vasodilation, and substantial accelerometer activity reflecting cyclical limb movement. Anaerobic exercise consists of short-duration, maximal-intensity bursts of physical effort, producing explosive accelerometer signals, rapid heart rate elevation, and pronounced sweat response.

Despite these distinct physiological signatures, automated classification remains challenging for several reasons. First, stress and aerobic exercise share the common phenotype of elevated heart rate, requiring multi-channel features to discriminate. Second, the wearable signals are recorded at different frequencies and must be temporally aligned before any window-level analysis is possible. Third, the substantial inter-subject physiological variability — in resting heart rate, baseline EDA, and skin temperature — means that classifiers trained on one individual may not generalize to another unless normalization is applied at the subject level.

This project addresses these challenges through a complete end-to-end pipeline: multi-rate signal alignment, subject-level normalization, sliding-window segmentation, physiologically grounded feature extraction, and rigorous cross-subject model evaluation. The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews related work on physiological state classification from wearable data. Section 3 formalizes the problem statement. Section 4 describes the full methodology. Section 5 presents the experimental evaluation. Section 6 discusses findings and limitations. Section 7 concludes with directions for future work.

2. Related Work¶

Research on physiological state classification from wearable data spans three primary areas: stress detection, exercise type recognition, and multimodal fusion for combined state discrimination.

2.1 Stress Detection from Physiological Signals¶

Electrodermal activity has been one of the most studied single-channel stress biomarkers due to its exclusive sympathetic nervous system innervation. Healey and Picard [1] demonstrated that EDA, combined with HR and EMG, could classify driver stress from a wearable device with greater than 97% accuracy in a controlled study. Schmidt et al. [2] introduced the WESAD dataset, a widely used benchmark for wearable stress and affect detection using the Empatica E4, establishing that EDA and BVP-derived features are the most discriminative channels for binary stress versus non-stress classification. Gjoreski et al. [3] showed that short-term HRV features, particularly RMSSD and pNN50, provide robust stress indicators when combined with EDA statistics, outperforming single-modality approaches.

2.2 Exercise State Recognition¶

Physical activity recognition from accelerometer data is a mature research area [4], with studies demonstrating that the magnitude, variance, and spectral energy of acceleration signals can reliably distinguish walking, running, cycling, and rest. For exercise intensity classification, Spierer et al. [5] showed that wrist-worn accelerometry combined with heart rate achieves high accuracy in classifying aerobic versus anaerobic effort. Parak and Korhonen [6] demonstrated that the combination of BVP-derived HR and accelerometry provides complementary information for distinguishing exercise intensity levels, with HRV features providing additional discriminative power at submaximal exercise levels.

2.3 Joint Stress and Exercise Classification¶

Fewer studies have tackled the combined discrimination of stress, aerobic exercise, and anaerobic exercise from a single wearable device. Reiss and Stricker [7] proposed a multi-sensor fusion approach for activity and affect recognition but did not include anaerobic exercise protocols. The dataset used in this project — introduced by Hongn et al. [8] — is specifically designed to address this gap, providing the first publicly available dataset with controlled stress induction and both aerobic and anaerobic exercise protocols recorded under the same wearable platform. The present work extends pilot binary classification results reported by the dataset authors [8] to the full three-class problem, with rigorous leave-one-subject-out evaluation.

References are listed in Section 8.

3. Problem Statement¶

Let $\mathcal{D} = \{(X_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}$ denote the full labeled dataset, where each sample $X_i \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times C}$ is a fixed-length time window of $T$ time steps across $C$ physiological signal channels, and $y_i \in \mathcal{Y} = \{\text{stress},\ \text{aerobic},\ \text{anaerobic}\}$ is the corresponding physiological state label.

The classification objective is to learn a function $f: \mathbb{R}^{T \times C} \rightarrow \mathcal{Y}$ that maps a multimodal sensor window to its physiological state label. In practice, $f$ operates on a feature vector $\phi(X_i) \in \mathbb{R}^d$ extracted from the raw window, where $\phi$ is a hand-designed physiological feature extractor.

Generalization Requirement¶

The primary scientific requirement is that $f$ generalizes to subjects not seen during training. This means that standard random train/test splits are insufficient — they allow windows from the same subject to appear in both the training and test sets, producing artificially inflated performance estimates. Instead, the evaluation protocol uses Leave-One-Subject-Out (LOSO) cross-validation, where in each fold $k$, the model is trained on all windows from subjects $\mathcal{S} \setminus \{s_k\}$ and evaluated on all windows from held-out subject $s_k$:

$$f_k = \underset{f}{\arg\min}\ \mathcal{L}\bigl(\{(X_i, y_i) : \text{subject}(X_i) \neq s_k\}\bigr)$$

Final performance is reported by aggregating predictions across all folds.

Dataset Summary¶

The dataset originates from three structured experimental sessions recorded using the Empatica E4 wristband:

Session Protocol # Subjects Duration
STRESS Stroop + Trier Mental Challenge tasks ~36 ~45 min
AEROBIC Storer-Davis incremental cycling protocol ~30 ~35 min
ANAEROBIC Wingate anaerobic sprint protocol (3–4 × 30-45s sprints) ~31 ~25 min

Each session folder contains one subfolder per subject, identified by cohort prefix (fXX for V2 cohort, SXX for V1 cohort). Some subjects have split sessions stored as _a / _b subfolders and are treated as independent records in this analysis.

Signal File Format¶

Every CSV file exported by the Empatica E4 follows a consistent two-row header convention:

  • Row 0: the session start time as a Unix UTC timestamp (float) or a human-readable datetime string.
  • Row 1: the native sampling rate of the signal in Hz (float).
  • Rows 2 onward: the signal samples. For ACC.csv there are three columns (x, y, z); all other files have a single value column.

The IBI.csv file is structurally distinct: row 0 holds the start timestamp, and subsequent rows contain pairs of [time_offset_from_start_in_seconds, IBI_duration_in_seconds]. Because heartbeats are aperiodic, IBI values cannot be assigned a fixed sampling rate and are retained as a sparse time-stamped table for use during HRV feature extraction.

The tags.csv file contains one UTC timestamp per row, each corresponding to a button press marking a protocol phase boundary. In this project, session-level labels are directly inferred from the folder structure (STRESS/AEROBIC/ANAEROBIC) rather than from tag-based intra-session segmentation.

The parse_timestamp helper handles both Unix float and datetime string formats uniformly. The load_signal function is fully wrapped in a try-except so that any unexpected file format returns None without crashing the pipeline.

def parse_timestamp(value):
    try:
        return float(value)
    except (ValueError, TypeError):
        return pd.Timestamp(str(value)).timestamp()


def load_signal(filepath):
    filepath = Path(filepath)
    if not filepath.exists():
        return None

    try:
        raw = pd.read_csv(filepath, header=None)

        if filepath.stem == "IBI":
            start_time = parse_timestamp(raw.iloc[0, 0])
            ibi = raw.iloc[1:].copy().reset_index(drop=True)

            if ibi.shape[1] < 2:
                return None

            ibi = ibi.iloc[:, :2].copy()
            ibi.columns = ["time_offset", "ibi"]
            ibi["time_offset"] = pd.to_numeric(ibi["time_offset"], errors="coerce")
            ibi["ibi"] = pd.to_numeric(ibi["ibi"], errors="coerce")
            ibi = ibi.dropna().reset_index(drop=True)

            if len(ibi) == 0:
                return None

            ibi["timestamp"] = start_time + ibi["time_offset"]
            return ibi[["timestamp", "ibi"]]

        start_time = parse_timestamp(raw.iloc[0, 0])
        sample_rate = float(raw.iloc[1, 0])
        values = raw.iloc[2:].reset_index(drop=True)

        if len(values) < 1:
            return None

        if filepath.stem == "ACC":
            values = values.iloc[:, :3].copy()
            values.columns = ["x", "y", "z"]

            for col in ["x", "y", "z"]:
                values[col] = pd.to_numeric(values[col], errors="coerce")

            values = values.dropna().reset_index(drop=True)
            if len(values) == 0:
                return None

            timestamps = start_time + np.arange(len(values)) / sample_rate

            df = pd.DataFrame({
                "timestamp": timestamps,
                "x": values["x"],
                "y": values["y"],
                "z": values["z"]
            })
            df["magnitude"] = np.sqrt(df["x"]**2 + df["y"]**2 + df["z"]**2)
            return df[["timestamp", "magnitude", "x", "y", "z"]]

        col = filepath.stem.lower()
        vals = pd.to_numeric(values.iloc[:, 0], errors="coerce")
        valid = vals.notna()

        if valid.sum() == 0:
            return None

        vals = vals[valid].reset_index(drop=True)
        timestamps = start_time + np.arange(len(vals)) / sample_rate

        return pd.DataFrame({
            "timestamp": timestamps,
            col: vals
        })

    except Exception:
        return None


def load_tags(filepath):
    filepath = Path(filepath)
    if not filepath.exists():
        return np.array([])

    try:
        raw = pd.read_csv(filepath, header=None)
        return np.array([parse_timestamp(v) for v in raw.iloc[:, 0].values])
    except Exception:
        return np.array([])


def load_subject(subject_path, label):
    subject_path = Path(subject_path)
    subject_id = subject_path.name
    base_subject_id = subject_id.replace("_a", "").replace("_b", "")

    record = {
        "subject_id": subject_id,
        "base_subject_id": base_subject_id,
        "label": label
    }

    for sig in ["BVP", "ACC", "EDA", "TEMP", "HR", "IBI"]:
        record[sig] = load_signal(subject_path / f"{sig}.csv")

    record["tags"] = load_tags(subject_path / "tags.csv")
    return record

4.4 Preprocessing¶

Signal Alignment¶

The five primary channels (BVP, EDA, TEMP, HR, ACC) are sampled at four distinct rates, making direct multi-channel analysis impossible without a common time axis. The alignment procedure applies three steps:

  1. Common interval detection. For each subject record, the latest start time and earliest end time across all available signal channels are identified. This defines the valid overlap interval during which all sensors were actively recording.
  2. Uniform time grid construction. A regular time grid at TARGET_FS = 4 Hz is created over the overlap interval. The target frequency of 4 Hz was selected because it matches the native rate of EDA and TEMP — the two channels most informative for stress discrimination — while still providing temporal resolution sufficient for motion and cardiovascular features over a 30-second window.
  3. Linear interpolation. Each channel is projected onto the common grid using np.interp. For signals slower than 4 Hz (HR at 1 Hz), this represents mild upsampling. For BVP at 64 Hz, this is a significant downsampling; this is acceptable because BVP is used exclusively through IBI-derived HRV features rather than raw waveform analysis. ACC magnitude is similarly downsampled, retaining motion intensity information.

Any record whose overlap interval is shorter than one window (30 seconds) is discarded.

Subject-Level Z-Score Normalization¶

After alignment, each channel of each subject's DataFrame is independently normalized to zero mean and unit variance using that subject's own statistics. This step eliminates inter-individual differences in absolute physiological baselines — resting heart rate, skin conductance level, and body temperature vary substantially across healthy adults — that would otherwise allow a classifier to partially learn subject identity rather than physiological state. Crucially, normalization is applied per subject before windowing, ensuring that the scaler sees only that subject's data and cannot introduce cross-subject information.

def align_signals(record, target_fs=TARGET_FS):
    signal_map = {}

    for sig_name in ["BVP", "EDA", "TEMP", "HR"]:
        df = record.get(sig_name)
        if df is not None and len(df) > 1:
            col = [c for c in df.columns if c != "timestamp"][0]
            df_clean = df[["timestamp", col]].dropna(subset=["timestamp"])
            if len(df_clean) > 1:
                signal_map[sig_name.lower()] = df_clean

    acc_df = record.get("ACC")
    if acc_df is not None and len(acc_df) > 1:
        acc_clean = acc_df[["timestamp", "magnitude"]].dropna(subset=["timestamp"])
        if len(acc_clean) > 1:
            signal_map["acc_mag"] = acc_clean.rename(columns={"magnitude": "acc_mag"})

    if len(signal_map) < 2:
        return None

    t_start = max(df["timestamp"].min() for df in signal_map.values())
    t_end   = min(df["timestamp"].max() for df in signal_map.values())

    if t_end - t_start < WINDOW_SEC:
        return None

    common_t = np.arange(t_start, t_end, 1.0 / target_fs)
    aligned  = pd.DataFrame({"timestamp": common_t})

    for col_name, df in signal_map.items():
        val_col = [c for c in df.columns if c != "timestamp"][0]
        aligned[col_name] = np.interp(
            common_t,
            df["timestamp"].values,
            df[val_col].values.astype(float)
        )

    return aligned


def normalize_signals(df):
    df = df.copy()
    for col in [c for c in df.columns if c != "timestamp"]:
        std = df[col].std()
        df[col] = (df[col] - df[col].mean()) / std if std > 1e-9 else 0.0
    return df

4.5 Segmentation and Label Assignment¶

Window Design¶

Each preprocessed, normalized session is segmented into fixed-length windows using a sliding window approach. The key design parameters are:

  • Window length: 30 seconds (120 samples at 4 Hz). This duration is the minimum recommended for reliable short-term HRV analysis, which requires enough heartbeats to produce statistically stable estimates of RMSSD and pNN50. A 30-second window at typical resting heart rate (~70 BPM) contains approximately 35 inter-beat intervals, which is sufficient for short-term HRV computation.
  • Overlap: 50% (15-second step, 60-sample stride). The 50% overlap increases the effective number of windows per session, reducing the impact of boundary artifacts and providing more training samples for the classifiers. It is standard practice in physiological time-series segmentation.

Windows shorter than the full 120 samples (arising at the end of a session) are discarded rather than zero-padded, to avoid introducing artificial signal discontinuities.

Label Assignment¶

Each window is assigned the session-level label of the subject from which it was extracted. This label is inherited directly from the folder structure established during data loading:

  • Windows from STRESS/ subjects → label stress
  • Windows from AEROBIC/ subjects → label aerobic
  • Windows from ANAEROBIC/ subjects → label anaerobic

The IBI table is passed alongside each window record so that HRV features can be extracted from the heartbeats that fall within the window's absolute timestamp range, independent of the fixed-rate resampling applied to the other channels.

In [61]:
WINDOW_SAMPLES = int(WINDOW_SEC * TARGET_FS)
STEP_SAMPLES = int(WINDOW_SAMPLES * (1 - OVERLAP_FRAC))

def create_windows(record):
    df = record["data"]
    label = record["label"]
    subject_id = record["subject_id"]
    base_subject_id = record["base_subject_id"]
    ibi = record.get("ibi")
    n = len(df)
    windows = []

    for start in range(0, n - WINDOW_SAMPLES + 1, STEP_SAMPLES):
        end = start + WINDOW_SAMPLES
        segment = df.iloc[start:end]

        windows.append({
            "subject_id": subject_id,
            "base_subject_id": base_subject_id,
            "label": label,
            "window": segment.reset_index(drop=True),
            "ibi": ibi,
            "t_start": float(segment["timestamp"].iloc[0]),
            "t_end": float(segment["timestamp"].iloc[-1]),
        })

    return windows

4.6 Feature Engineering¶

Rather than using raw signal arrays as classifier input, each 30-second window is transformed into a compact, physiologically interpretable feature vector. This approach is appropriate for classical machine learning models and produces features that are directly interpretable in terms of known stress and exercise physiology.

Heart Rate Variability (HRV) from IBI¶

HRV measures the fluctuation in the time between consecutive heartbeats and is a well-established index of autonomic nervous system balance. Parasympathetic dominance (rest) produces high HRV; sympathetic activation (stress, intense exercise) suppresses it. Four standard time-domain HRV metrics are computed from the IBI intervals whose timestamps fall within each window's absolute time range:

Feature Description
hrv_rmssd Root mean square of successive IBI differences — most sensitive to short-term parasympathetic modulation
hrv_sdnn Standard deviation of all IBIs — reflects overall autonomic variability
hrv_mean_hr Mean heart rate in BPM, derived as 60 / mean(IBI)
hrv_pnn50 Percentage of successive differences > 50 ms — a robust parasympathetic index

If fewer than 3 IBI values fall within a window's time range (e.g., due to signal dropout), all HRV features are set to NaN and subsequently imputed with the column median.

Electrodermal Activity (EDA)¶

EDA is driven exclusively by eccrine sweat glands under sympathetic nervous system control, making it the most specific non-cardiac biomarker of psychological arousal. Five features capture the amplitude and trajectory of the EDA signal within the window: mean, standard deviation, minimum, maximum, and linear slope. The slope is particularly informative because EDA rises during stress onset and decays slowly during recovery.

Accelerometer Magnitude (ACC)¶

The three-axis acceleration magnitude summarizes total body movement intensity. Mean and standard deviation capture average and variable motion; energy (sum of squared values) captures burst-type activity characteristic of anaerobic sprints; maximum captures peak acceleration; slope captures whether motion is accelerating or decelerating.

Skin Temperature (TEMP)¶

Temperature responds slowly and is most informative for distinguishing aerobic exercise (progressive thermoregulatory rise) from stress (possible vasoconstriction at the periphery). Mean, standard deviation, and slope are computed.

Blood Volume Pulse (BVP) and Heart Rate (HR)¶

BVP mean, standard deviation, and amplitude range complement the IBI-derived HR features. HR mean, standard deviation, and slope characterize the cardiovascular response level and trajectory.

The complete feature vector for each window has 25 dimensions, concatenating contributions from all five modalities.

def safe_slope(series):
    if series is None or len(series) < 2:
        return 0.0
    x = np.arange(len(series), dtype=float)
    s = series.values.astype(float)
    return float(np.polyfit(x, s, 1)[0])


def hrv_features(ibi_data, t_start, t_end):
    nan_out = {
        "hrv_rmssd": np.nan,
        "hrv_sdnn": np.nan,
        "hrv_mean_hr": np.nan,
        "hrv_pnn50": np.nan,
    }

    if ibi_data is None or len(ibi_data) == 0:
        return nan_out

    mask = (ibi_data["timestamp"] >= t_start) & (ibi_data["timestamp"] <= t_end)
    ibis = ibi_data.loc[mask, "ibi"].values.astype(float)

    if len(ibis) < 3:
        return nan_out

    diffs = np.diff(ibis)

    return {
        "hrv_rmssd": float(np.sqrt(np.mean(diffs ** 2))),
        "hrv_sdnn": float(np.std(ibis, ddof=1)),
        "hrv_mean_hr": float(60.0 / np.mean(ibis)) if np.mean(ibis) > 0 else np.nan,
        "hrv_pnn50": float(100.0 * np.mean(np.abs(diffs) > 0.05)),
    }


def eda_features(series):
    return {
        "eda_mean": float(series.mean()),
        "eda_std": float(series.std()),
        "eda_min": float(series.min()),
        "eda_max": float(series.max()),
        "eda_slope": safe_slope(series),
    }


def acc_features(series):
    s = series.values.astype(float)
    return {
        "acc_mean": float(series.mean()),
        "acc_std": float(series.std()),
        "acc_energy": float(np.sum(s ** 2)),
        "acc_max": float(series.max()),
        "acc_slope": safe_slope(series),
    }


def temp_features(series):
    return {
        "temp_mean": float(series.mean()),
        "temp_std": float(series.std()),
        "temp_slope": safe_slope(series),
    }


def bvp_features(series):
    return {
        "bvp_mean": float(series.mean()),
        "bvp_std": float(series.std()),
        "bvp_range": float(series.max() - series.min()),
    }


def hr_features(series):
    return {
        "hr_mean": float(series.mean()),
        "hr_std": float(series.std()),
        "hr_slope": safe_slope(series),
    }


def extract_features(win_record):
    w = win_record["window"]

    row = {
        "subject_id": win_record["subject_id"],

5. Experimental Evaluation¶

5.1 Experimental Setup¶

Validation Protocol: Leave-One-Subject-Out Cross-Validation¶

All models are evaluated under Leave-One-Subject-Out (LOSO) cross-validation. In each of $K$ folds (one per unique subject identifier), the held-out subject's windows form the test set, and windows from all remaining subjects form the training set. A fresh StandardScaler is fit on the training feature matrix in each fold and applied to both train and test sets — this is critical because it ensures that no test-subject statistics are used during normalization, which would constitute a form of data leakage.

Class imbalance is addressed by passing class_weight='balanced' to all sklearn classifiers, which reweights the loss proportionally to the inverse class frequency in the training fold. This prevents classifiers from defaulting to the majority class when window counts per class differ across session lengths.

Computing Environment¶

All experiments run on a standard Python 3.9 environment with scikit-learn ≥ 1.2, numpy ≥ 1.23, and pandas ≥ 1.5. No GPU is required.

5.2 Models¶

Three classifiers are included in the comparison:

Logistic Regression (LR) is the linear baseline. It fits a multinomial logistic model via L-BFGS optimization with L2 regularization (C=1.0 by default). Its decision boundary is a linear hyperplane in the 25-dimensional feature space; misclassifications reveal when the two most similar classes (typically stress and aerobic) overlap linearly.

Random Forest (RF) is an ensemble of 150 decision trees, each trained on a bootstrap sample and a random subset of features. The ensemble vote aggregates tree predictions, reducing variance and handling non-linear feature interactions that are invisible to logistic regression. Feature importances, measured by mean decrease in Gini impurity averaged across all trees and LOSO folds, provide a ranking of the most discriminative physiological features.

Gradient Boosting (GB) builds an additive ensemble of 150 shallow trees (max depth 4), where each successive tree fits the pseudo-residuals of the current ensemble. This iterative residual correction allows it to model complex decision boundaries and typically achieves the highest accuracy on structured tabular physiological data, at the cost of longer training time.

5.3 Evaluation Metrics¶

Performance is quantified using five complementary metrics:

  • Accuracy: fraction of windows correctly classified across all classes and all held-out folds. Provides an overall success rate but can mask per-class imbalance.
  • Precision (macro): unweighted mean of per-class precision values. Precision for a class $c$ is $TP_c / (TP_c + FP_c)$ — the fraction of windows predicted as class $c$ that truly belong to class $c$. A low precision indicates many false alarms for that class.
  • Recall (macro): unweighted mean of per-class recall values. Recall for class $c$ is $TP_c / (TP_c + FN_c)$ — the fraction of true class $c$ windows that were correctly detected.
  • F1 Macro: harmonic mean of macro precision and recall. The macro average treats all classes equally, making it the most appropriate summary metric when all three physiological states are equally important.
  • F1 Weighted: frequency-weighted F1, accounting for class size differences across sessions.

The confusion matrix visualizes the full pattern of correct and incorrect classifications. Values are normalized as percentages of each true class (row-normalized), so the matrix is interpretable regardless of class size differences.

6. Discussion¶

6.1 Interpretation of Results¶

The confusion matrices and per-class F1 scores reveal consistent patterns across all three classifiers that are directly explainable by the underlying physiology.

Anaerobic exercise is systematically the best-classified state. The Wingate sprint protocol produces sharp, high-amplitude acceleration bursts that are unique to maximal-effort physical activity. The acc_energy and acc_max features capture this explosive signature in a way that neither stress nor aerobic exercise replicates. Any model — including the linear baseline — can reliably separate anaerobic windows from the other two classes using motion features alone.

Aerobic exercise benefits from a combination of sustained motion, elevated heart rate, and rising skin temperature that distinguishes it from stress at the sensor level. The hr_mean, temp_slope, and acc_std features are expected to contribute most to aerobic classification, and their presence in the feature importance rankings confirms this. Confusions between aerobic and anaerobic arise because both involve physical exertion; the distinguishing signal is the temporal profile of the activity (sustained vs. burst) rather than its absolute intensity.

Stress is the most challenging class because it shares elevated heart rate with aerobic exercise and elevated EDA with both exercise types, without a unique single-channel signature. Classification relies on the combination of suppressed HRV (hrv_rmssd, hrv_pnn50), elevated EDA, and low accelerometer activity — it is the absence of motion combined with autonomic arousal that defines stress. This multivariate pattern is learnable by ensemble models but partially invisible to logistic regression's linear boundary.

The performance gap between Logistic Regression and the tree-based methods reflects precisely this non-linearity. The conjunction of features required to identify stress (high EDA and low ACC and low HRV) can only be represented by a conjunction in a decision tree, not by a weighted sum. Random Forest and Gradient Boosting are able to capture such interactions, which is reflected in their higher macro F1 scores.

6.2 Limitations¶

Inter-cohort protocol differences. The V1 (SXX) and V2 (fXX) subject cohorts followed different versions of the stress and exercise protocols. V2 extended rest periods, removed the Stroop task, and introduced a group exercise setting. These differences may introduce systematic distributional shifts between cohort subgroups. A classifier evaluated under LOSO may therefore be tested on subjects from a different protocol version than those in its training set, potentially inflating variance.

Session-level labeling without intra-session phase discrimination. The current pipeline assigns a single label to the entire session, but real sessions contain multiple phases — for the stress protocol, these include baseline recording, active stress tasks, and rest periods. Windows from rest periods that occur between stress tasks are labeled as "stress" even though the subject's physiology may be partially recovered. This label noise is a source of systematic error, particularly for the stress class.

Subject pool size. With approximately 75–85 unique records after exclusions, the LOSO evaluation has high variance: the performance reported for any single subject as the held-out test set depends heavily on whether that subject's physiological response falls within the range captured by the remaining training subjects. Larger subject pools would reduce this variance.

7. Conclusion¶

This project presented a complete, end-to-end pipeline for classifying acute stress, aerobic exercise, and anaerobic exercise from multimodal wearable sensor data, evaluated under rigorous leave-one-subject-out cross-validation. The pipeline addressed the four core technical challenges of the dataset — heterogeneous sampling rates, session-level label assignment, inter-subject physiological variability, and known data quality issues in specific subjects — through principled signal alignment, subject-level normalization, sliding-window segmentation, and explicit exclusion of flagged records.

The feature engineering pipeline computed 25 physiologically grounded features across five signal modalities, with HRV metrics from IBI, EDA statistics, ACC motion features, temperature dynamics, and BVP/HR summaries each contributing distinct discriminative information. Ensemble methods (Random Forest and Gradient Boosting) substantially outperformed the linear baseline in classifying the stress class, where the discriminative signal is a conjunction of features rather than a linear combination. Anaerobic exercise proved to be the most reliably classified state due to the distinctive high-energy accelerometer signature of maximal sprint effort.

Future Directions¶

Several extensions could substantially improve this framework:

  1. Intra-session phase segmentation using tags.csv. Using the protocol event markers to exclude rest and transition periods, and to restrict labeled windows to the active phases of each protocol, would reduce label noise and improve stress classification performance.
  2. Temporal deep learning. A 1D Convolutional Neural Network or LSTM trained directly on the 4 Hz aligned signal arrays could capture temporal dynamics — such as the rising slope of EDA during stress onset or the rhythmic pattern of aerobic cycling — that summary statistics discard.
  3. Multimodal late fusion. Training separate models per modality and combining their predictions through a meta-classifier would reveal which channels are individually most informative and whether modality combination provides complementary benefit.
  4. Expanded cohort. Including participants from diverse age groups, fitness levels, and clinical populations would test whether the learned feature representations generalize beyond the healthy young adult population from which the current dataset was drawn.
